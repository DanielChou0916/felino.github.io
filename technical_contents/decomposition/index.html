Tension-Compression Asymmetry

The mechanical responses of materials under tension and compression are usually distitable, in order to capture the tension-compression asymmetry(so call anisotropic response), The energy decomposition is adopted, here. In practice, stress or strain are decomposed into activated part \((\cdot)^+\) and inactivated part \((\cdot)^-\), by certain rule. Then the elastic energy \(\psi^+\) corresponding to activated part is included in to phase-field governing equation.

  • Spectral / Deviatoric Decomposition
  • Representative Crack Element (RCE)
  • Drucker-Prager Decomposition
  • Extra Driving Force Formulation

Spectral / Deviatoric Decomposition

In asymmetric response, elastic energy is split into activated (tensile) and inactivated (compressive) components. The total strain energy density is written

\[ \psi_s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon},d) = \omega(d)\,\psi_e^+(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) + \psi_e^-(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}), \]

where the tensile and compressive parts are defined via spectral decomposition of the infinitesimal strain

\[ \psi_e^\pm(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) = \frac12\,\lambda\,\langle\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon})\rangle_\pm^2 + \mu \sum_a \langle \epsilon_a\rangle_\pm^2, \quad \langle x\rangle_\pm = \frac{x\pm|x|}{2}. \]

Activated energy \(\psi_e^+\) is responsible for driving crack growth, while inactivated energy \(\psi_e^-\) remains dormant under compression. A temporal derivative highlights how damage couples to energy:

\[ \dot{\psi}_s = \bigl(\omega(d)\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_0^+ + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_0^-\bigr) : \dot{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} - Y\,\dot d, \quad Y = -\,\psi_e^+\,\frac{\mathrm{d}\omega}{\mathrm{d}d}. \]

Representative Crack Element (RCE)

Asymmetric fracture is approximated by interpolating between the intact elastic state \(\psi_0\) and a fully cracked state \(\psi_c\):

\[ \psi_{\mathrm{elastic}} = \omega(d)\,\bigl(\psi_0 - \psi_c\bigr) + \psi_c. \]

The strain jump that activates fracture is computed via directional projections

\[ \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathrm{jump}} = \sum_{i=1}^3 \Gamma_i\,\mathbf{P}_i, \quad \Delta\boldsymbol{\epsilon} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathrm{jump}}. \]

Residual (inactivated) and activated energies follow as

\[ \psi_{\mathrm{res}} = \tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{res}} : \Delta\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \psi_{\mathrm{act}} = \tfrac12\,\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{act}} : \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathrm{jump}}, \]

where

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{res}} = \mathbb{C} : \Delta\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\mathrm{act}} = \mathbb{C} : \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathrm{jump}}. \]

Drucker-Prager Decomposition

Asymmetric pressure-sensitive behavior is achieved by splitting strain energy via a Drucker-Prager criterion. The positive and negative parts are defined piecewise to enforce a yield surface on invariants:

\[ I_1 = \mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}), \quad J_2 = \tfrac12\,\mathrm{tr}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathrm{dev}}^2\bigr), \quad B = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\frac{\sigma_t - \sigma_c}{\sigma_t + \sigma_c}. \]

The inactivated and activated energies are then given by:

\[ \psi_e^- = \begin{cases} 0, & I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2} > 0,\\[0.5em] \dfrac{K\mu}{18B^2K + 2\mu}\bigl(I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2}\bigr)^2, & \ldots,\\[0.5em] \tfrac12K\,I_1^2 + 2\mu\,J_2, & \ldots, \end{cases} \quad \psi_e^+ = \begin{cases} \tfrac12K\,I_1^2 + 2\mu\,J_2, & I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2} > 0,\\[0.5em] \dfrac{(-3KB\,I_1 + 2\mu\sqrt{J_2})^2}{18B^2K + 2\mu}, & \ldots,\\[0.5em] 0, & \ldots. \end{cases} \]

Pressure-dependent degradation of both tensile and compressive strengths is thus enforced, yielding an asymmetric response under varying hydrostatic stress states.

Extra Driving Force Formulation

Additional driving force \(c_e\) is introduced to model asymmetric compressive-shear behavior, especially for geomaterials. Governing equation becomes:

\[ \omega'(d)\,\psi_e(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) + \frac{G_c}{c_0\,\ell}\,\alpha'(d) + c_e - \nabla\!\cdot\!\Bigl(\tfrac{2G_c\,\ell}{c_0}\,\nabla d\Bigr) = 0. \]

The term \(c_e\) is a function of stress/strain invariants and material strengths (tensile strength \(\sigma_t\), compressive strength \(\sigma_c\), crack-width coefficient), providing extra resistance under compression and shear.
