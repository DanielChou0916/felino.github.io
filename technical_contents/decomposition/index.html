Tension-Compression Asymmetry

Materials often respond very differently when loaded in tension versus compression. To capture this tension-compression asymmetry (sometimes called an "anisotropic" response), the strain or stress is split into an activated (tensile) part \((\cdot)^+\) and an inactivated (compressive) part \((\cdot)^-\). Only the activated energy \(\Psi^+\) drives crack growth in the phase‐field equation.

Spectral / Deviatoric Decomposition

Split the total elastic energy into activated (tensile) and inactivated (compressive) parts:

\[ \Psi_s(\epsilon, d) = \omega(d)\,\Psi_e^+(\epsilon) + \Psi_e^-(\epsilon)\,, \]

where the classical spectral (eigenvalue) split of infinitesimal strain \(\epsilon\) gives

\[ \Psi_e^\pm(\epsilon) = \tfrac12\,\lambda\,\langle \mathrm{tr}(\epsilon)\rangle_\pm^2 + \mu \sum_a \langle \epsilon_a\rangle_\pm^2, \quad \langle x\rangle_\pm = \tfrac{x\pm|x|}{2}. \]

Here \(\langle\epsilon_a\rangle_+\) picks out tensile principal strains and \(\langle\epsilon_a\rangle_-\) the compressive ones. The activated energy \(\Psi_e^+\) powers crack driving, while \(\Psi_e^-\) remains dormant under compression. A time derivative of \(\Psi_s\) shows how damage couples to energy release:

\[ \dot\Psi_s = \bigl(\omega(d)\,\sigma_0^+ + \sigma_0^-\bigr)\!:\!\dot\epsilon \;-\; Y\,\dot d, \quad Y = -\,\Psi_e^+\,\frac{d\omega}{dd}\,, \]

where \(\sigma_0^\pm = \partial\Psi_e^\pm/\partial\epsilon\) are the stress contributions from each part and \(Y\) is the fracture driving force.

Representative Crack Element (RCE)

Approximate the asymmetric response by interpolating between an intact state \(\Psi_0\) and a fully cracked state \(\Psi_c\):

\[ \Psi_{\mathrm{elastic}} = \omega(d)\,\bigl(\Psi_0 - \Psi_c\bigr) + \Psi_c. \]

Decompose the strain into a “jump” component that activates fracture:

\[ \epsilon_{\mathrm{jump}} = \sum_{i=1}^3 \Gamma_i\,P_i, \quad \Delta\epsilon = \epsilon - \epsilon_{\mathrm{jump}}. \]

Then define residual (inactivated) and activated energies via their stresses:

\[ \Psi_{\mathrm{res}} = \tfrac12\,\sigma_{\mathrm{res}} : \Delta\epsilon, \quad \Psi_{\mathrm{act}} = \tfrac12\,\sigma_{\mathrm{act}} : \epsilon_{\mathrm{jump}}, \]

with

\[ \sigma_{\mathrm{res}} = \mathbb{C} : \Delta\epsilon, \quad \sigma_{\mathrm{act}} = \mathbb{C} : \epsilon_{\mathrm{jump}}. \]

Drucker-Prager Decomposition

Enforce pressure-dependent asymmetry by splitting energy based on the invariants

\[ I_1 = \mathrm{tr}(\epsilon), \quad J_2 = \tfrac12\,\mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(\epsilon_{\mathrm{dev}}^2\bigr), \quad B = \tfrac1{\sqrt3}\,\frac{\sigma_t-\sigma_c}{\sigma_t+\sigma_c}. \]

Then piecewise define inactivated and activated energies to impose the Drucker-Prager yield:

\[ \Psi_e^- = \begin{cases} 0, & I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2} > 0,\\[0.5em] \dfrac{K\mu}{18B^2K + 2\mu}\,\bigl(I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2}\bigr)^2, & \ldots,\\[0.5em] \tfrac12K\,I_1^2 + 2\mu\,J_2, & \ldots, \end{cases} \quad \Psi_e^+ = \begin{cases} \tfrac12K\,I_1^2 + 2\mu\,J_2, & I_1 + 6B\sqrt{J_2} > 0,\\[0.5em] \dfrac{\bigl(-3KB\,I_1 + 2\mu\sqrt{J_2}\bigr)^2}{18B^2K + 2\mu}, & \ldots,\\[0.5em] 0, & \ldots. \end{cases} \]

This enforces different degradation under tension and compression, capturing pressure‐sensitive fracture behavior.

Extra Driving Force Formulation

Add an extra term \(c_e\) to model compressive‐shear resistance in geomaterials. The phase‐field equation becomes:

\[ \omega'(d)\,\Psi_e(\epsilon) + \frac{G_c}{c_0\,l}\,\alpha'(d) + c_e - \nabla\!\cdot\!\Bigl(\tfrac{2G_c\,l}{c_0}\,\nabla d\Bigr) = 0. \]

Here \(c_e\) depends on stress/strain invariants and material strengths (\(\sigma_t\), \(\sigma_c\), crack‐width coefficient), providing extra resistance under compression and shear.
